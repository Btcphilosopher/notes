Quantity Theory of Money Executive Summary The Quantity Theory of Money (QTM) links money supply to price levels via the identity MV = PY, where M is money supply, V is velocity of circulation, P the price level and Y real output . Under classical assumptions (stable V, full-employment output), a proportional increase in M implies a proportional rise in P . Originating with thinkers like Locke and Hume in the 17–18th centuries, it was formalised by Fisher (1911) and Cambridge economists (Marshall, Pigou) in the early 20th century . Monetarists (Friedman) revived QTM in the 1950s–70s, encapsulated in the phrase “inflation is everywhere a monetary phenomenon” . However, Keynesian and modern schools (endogenous money, unstable velocity) have questioned its assumptions . This report rigorously examines QTM’s formulations, variants (classical, Cambridge, monetarist, modern), empirical evidence (cross-country and time-series, inflation-targeting era, hyperinflations) with key studies and data, critiques (velocity shocks, money endogeneity, financial innovation) and central-bank implications (monetary targeting vs inflation targeting, unconventional policies). We include tables summarising theory variants and empirical findings, a timeline of developments, and sample numerical models. Seminal sources (Fisher 1911; Friedman 1956, 1963; Keynes 1936; Jung 2024; Benati 2021, etc.) are cited throughout and listed for further reading. Definition and History The QTM posits that nominal spending (price × real output, PY) equals money supply × velocity: [M \times V = P \times Y.] For example, if money grows while V and Y are fixed, then P must rise proportionally . Fisher’s 1911 equation of exchange was MV = PT (with T transactions, often approximated by output) . The Cambridge cash-balance approach instead writes M = kPY, where k (cash-to-income ratio) is roughly constant . Both imply long-run monetary neutrality: changes in M affect P but not Y. Historically, Locke (1691) first asserted P ∝ M , and Hume (1752) explicitly argued that variations in M cause proportional price changes . Classical economists (Ricardo, J.S. Mill) accepted this in static form. In the 1920s, Irving Fisher formalized the transactions view (MV = PT) , while at Cambridge Marshall and Pigou emphasised money demand (M = kPY) . Keynes (1936) later critiqued QTM, calling velocity unstable and arguing money might be “hoarded” (liquidity preference) . Milton Friedman (1956) restated QTM as a long-run theory, famously noting “inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon” . This shaped 1970s policy (Volcker’s Fed monetarism ). If V (velocity) and Y are constant (or trend-stable), then ΔP ≈ ΔM. Classical theory assumes full employment, so real output Y is fixed and the stock of money determines the price level . This implies long-run monetary neutrality: money shocks affect only nominal variables. The Cambridge cash-balance variant instead models money demand: Mᴅ = kPᴅY, where k (=1/V) is stable . Equilibrium requires M = Mᴅ, yielding the same proportionality. Deriving V or k relies on institutional or behavioural assumptions (payment patterns, interest rates, etc.). Example (Illustrative): Suppose an economy has real GDP Y = 100 and velocity V = 5. Then MV = PY gives 100·5 = P·100, so P = 5 (price level). If M then rises to 110 (a 10% increase), with V and Y fixed, we have 110·5 = P'·100, so P' = 5.5 (a 10% inflation). In other words, P scales one-for-one with M . (If instead using M = kPY, an increase in M similarly raises P proportionally if k and Y are constant.) The QTM assumes money is exogenously controlled, velocity is stable, and prices adjust fully. Fisher in 1911 assumed velocity was well-defined and stable enough to make MV = PT useful . In practice, V often varies with interest rates, payment tech, and shocks. Modern formulations (monetarist Friedman) account for expected inflation affecting money demand, and allow for short-run price stickiness (so money can temporarily affect output). Theoretical Variants Classical Quantity Theory (Fisher): Focuses on transactions. Equation MV = PT or MV = PY . Assumes fixed velocity and output in the long run, so P ∝ M. Often sees money as a veil. Proponents: David Hume, Irving Fisher, 19th-century classics. Cambridge Cash-Balance (Pigou): Focuses on money as store-of-value. Equation M = kPY (with k = cash-income ratio) . Emphasises demand for real balances M/P. Assumes k roughly constant (so again P ∝ M). Useful for explaining why people hold money. Proponents: Marshall, Pigou, Robertson. Keynesian/Liquidity-Preference: Rejects simple proportionality. Money demand depends on income and interest rate (speculative motive). Short-run prices/wages may be sticky, so money has real effects. Money supply is partly endogenous (bank credit matters). Often downplays stable velocity . Key concept: liquidity trap (injections into idle hoards). Monetarist (Friedman): Reinstates QTM as a long-run theory. Friedman’s restatement uses MV = PY but treats velocity as predictable rather than constant. Key tenets: long-run neutrality, a “constant money growth” policy rule (grow M at steady rate) , and the assertion that inflation arises from money growth in excess of output growth . Emphasises empirical money demand stability at low frequencies. Modern (New Classical/Rational Expectations): Builds on QTM neutrality but with rational expectations. Money has no long-run effects (Walrasian models), and even short-run effects vanish if agents anticipate policy. The real financial system is often abstracted away. Typically central banks set an interest rate rule (e.g. Taylor rule) rather than targeting money, making M endogenous. Variants include the monetary theory of the exchange rate (monetary approach) and models where P dynamics follow money via a lag (Friedman-Phelps Phillips curve). Post-Keynesian/Endogenous Money: Argued (Moore, Kaldor) that money is created by bank lending, so M is endogenous to credit demand, not independently controlled. QTM is largely rejected. Some versions highlight roles of finance and distribution over money quantity . Empirical Evidence Cross-country and long-run studies: Early studies (e.g. Rolnick & Weber 1983) found strong cross-sectional correlations between average money growth and inflation over 1960–80 (under Fiat money) . Recent panel analyses confirm a high long-run elasticity: Benati (2021) finds that across 27 countries from the 18th century onward, broad money growth and inflation move roughly one-for-one in the very long run . ECB working papers (Barigozzi 2009, Jung 2024) using spectral and panel cointegration techniques find that the long-run component of money growth explains a large fraction of inflation variance , especially during episodes of monetary stress. For example, during the World Wars and the mid-20th-century “Great Inflation,” money and prices grew in lockstep , consistent with QTM. Time-series (single-country) studies: In the U.S. 19th–20th century, Friedman & Schwartz (1963) famously argued that Fed-induced money contractions deepened the 1930s Depression, implying a potent money–output link (subject to debate). During the 1970s, high US and UK money growth coincided with surging inflation, reinforcing monetarist claims. However, since the 1980s–90s, the empirical link weakened. Unit-root and cointegration tests often reject a stable proportionality at business-cycle frequencies . For example, Eichengreen et al. (2016) report that inflation-targeting central banks have very low money–inflation correlations in data, though a long-run cointegrating slope can still be estimated . Inflation-targeting era: After the 1980s, many studies note a “break” in the QTM relationship . Jung (2024) finds that in a panel of 18 countries, the slope of money vs inflation (the “gain”) fell markedly post-1985, only moving towards unity during rare high-inflation episodes . Benati (2021) similarly finds that from mid-1980s onward, the short-run correlation is weak, although a one-for-one long-run association remains recoverable via time-varying VARs . Practically, in the post-2008 era (quantitative easing), enormous base-money expansion caused little CPI inflation . Banque de France researchers note that since 2008 the rapid increase in money has not been accompanied by a corresponding rise in prices – consistent with a collapse of the simple QTM link in recent decades. Hyperinflations and historical case studies: Episodes of hyperinflation provide dramatic evidence for QTM. For example, Friedman (1963) notes that price levels in Weimar Germany, Hungary and other cases rose almost exactly with the exploding money supply . Cagan’s classic study (1956) showed that real money balances collapsed as inflation soared in these episodes. Sargent & Wallace (1981) demonstrate that stopping hyperinflation required halting money creation (and often stabilising debt). In short, almost all documented hyperinflations were triggered by runaway monetary financing of deficits (governments printing money) – a textbook QTM scenario. In many modern samples, rising money did not lead to immediate inflation . Critiques and Limitations Key criticisms highlight that QTM’s assumptions often fail in practice. Variable velocity: Velocity (V or 1/k) is not constant but changes with payment habits, interest rates, regulations and expectations. Keynes (1936) argued that V is unstable – a rise in money might simply reduce velocity (e.g. extra money is hoarded) . Empirical studies (e.g. [59]) show that in low-inflation regimes velocity tends to trend down, obscuring the money–inflation link. Endogeneity of money: In most economies, the monetary base and M1/M2 are largely determined by bank lending and money-demand factors, not directly by central-bank directives. The post-Keynesian view is that M adjusts to the needs of creditworthy borrowers . Thus, causality may run from demand for credit to M (and possibly inflation) rather than vice versa. Role of credit and financial innovation: Modern financial systems create many liquid assets outside traditional “money.” Credit creation by banks effectively increases money. Some argue that broad credit aggregates (bank loans) may drive inflation more than narrow M. However, recent long-run studies find that, since WWII, inflation’s correlation with credit growth is not stronger than with monetary aggregates . Nevertheless, deregulation, shadow banking and fintech can weaken the link between central-bank money and overall liquidity. For example, post-2008 quantitative easing massively expanded bank reserves, but these did not translate into broad money or spending because banks held excess reserves . In effect, the money multiplier fell (see [65] Fig. C2), so M did not rise proportionally. Interest rates and nominal anchors: QTM treats the price level mechanically, but in practice inflation expectations and policy regimes matter. In open economies, exchange-rate regimes (gold standard, Bretton Woods) and international flows also influenced domestic price levels. Moreover, modern central banks target inflation or interest rates (via Taylor rules), not money, making M endogenous. Finally, in the short run, sticky wages/prices can lead to money expansion fueling output rather than prices (the “nonneutrality” Keynes noted ). In sum, critics argue that QTM works best as a long-run approximation, and even then can be disrupted by velocity shocks or structural changes . Policy Implications for Central Banks If the QTM holds strictly, the prescription is clear: control money growth to stabilize prices. Monetarists (Friedman) advocated rules such as targeting a constant M–growth equal to real GDP growth . Indeed, in the late 1970s Volcker’s Fed temporarily focused on money aggregates (see timeline ). However, once velocity became volatile in the 1980s, many central banks abandoned strict money targets. In inflation-targeting regimes, policy is set by inflation forecasts and output gaps, largely ignoring M (money is endogenous in econometric models ). Instead, interest rates adjust to meet targets. Nonetheless, QTM still underpins some official thinking. Even inflation-targeting banks monitor money growth as a cross-check: unexplained surges in M might signal future inflationary pressure. Conversely, deflations are seen as reflecting excessive money contraction. Some analysts argue for a “money-poly” rule or nominal GDP targeting (which is a hybrid of MV targeting). Unconventional policy (QE, helicopter drops) poses new questions. Large-scale asset purchases expand M₀ (base money) enormously. According to simple QTM this should cause inflation; yet experience shows little immediate price rise . Central banks explain this by endogenous money: QE acts mainly through lower yields (not by increasing lending until conditions normalize). Policies like negative rates (pushing money into assets) and potential digital currencies further complicate the money–price nexus. In hyperinflations, however, classical QTM policy applies: only draconian monetary restraint (often with fiscal reform) brings inflation under control (see Sargent 1982). In summary, policy implications depend on one’s faith in QTM. A strict Quantity Theorist will emphasize money-supply rules and caution against money-financed deficits . A Keynesian might instead use open-mouth operations, interest guidance or fiscal policy to manage demand, viewing money as an intermediate tool. Most central banks today adopt a pragmatic mix: their official regime is often interest-rate targeting (with explicit or implicit inflation targets), but they keep an eye on money growth as a background indicator (for example, BoE and ECB still publish monetary analyses). Illustrative Models and Examples To illustrate QTM mechanisms, consider simple algebraic or log-linear models. For instance, a Camerbridge-k money-demand model: [ \frac{M}{P} = kY \quad\Rightarrow\quad M = kPY. ] If k = 0.25 and Y = 100, then M = 25P. A doubling of M (from 25 to 50) leads to doubling of P (from 1 to 2). Alternatively, a cash-in-advance model (used in modern theory) assumes consumers must finance purchases P·Y from pre-existing money M, so that M = P·Y. In both cases, only money growth changes P in equilibrium (assuming no change in Y). Numerical scenario: Suppose Y grows at 2% annually. A central bank sets M growth also at 2%. With velocity constant, inflation will be near 0%. If instead the bank pumps M at 7% (five points above output growth), the extra growth shows up as ~5% inflation in the long run. This is the essence of the “quantity rule” . One can also embed QTM in a dynamic IS–LM or New Keynesian model. For example, an IS-LM with a fixed velocity yields a Phillips-curve inflation of $\pi = (\Delta M/M) + \pi_{(-1)} - \alpha(u - u^*)$, etc. The precise mechanism (via expectations, money demand, and output gaps) is elaborated in advanced texts (e.g. Friedman 1968; Woodford 2003). The key insight remains that, all else equal, a higher money growth path shifts the Phillips curve up (higher inflation for a given output gap) in long-run projections.
